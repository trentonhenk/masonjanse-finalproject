Redlining and Environmental Health

By Mason Janse

Research

Using the historic maps drawn by the FHA in Austin, we can overlay current Human Health data and see some shocking differences in the redlined neighborhoods.

There are some very clear trends seen in this chart. Even in our own backyard, the legacy of redlining and the health disparities it has caused are still plaguing our city and our vulnerable communities. Because of the proximity to superfund and toxic facilities, highways, and lack of green space, we have built an unfair and unhealthy city. Austinâ€™s redlined residents have a life expectancy of 10 years less than their greenlined counterparts. Many of the illnesses are the exact culprits discussed over and over again with environmental health pollutants. Cancers, respiratory issues, and heart disease are all on the list. Although concentrated poverty and lack of access to healthcare are also contributors to lower life expectancy, environmental toxins seem to be a likely culprit.

Write our State representatives to advocate for change!

It is important for policymakers and community leaders in Austin and other cities to address the legacy of redlining and its impacts on environmental health. This can include investing in affordable housing and healthy food options in redlined neighborhoods, fixing superfund zoning, incentivizing cleaner transportation, testing water and air in these areas regularly, as well as implementing policies to prevent discrimination in the housing and lending markets. Only by addressing the root causes of inequality and poor health can we create a more equitable and healthy city for all residents of Austin.
