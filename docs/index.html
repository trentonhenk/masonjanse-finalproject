Redlining and Environmental Health

By Mason Janse

Introduction

Pollution does not affect all people the same. In the United States, urban, redlined communities are at a significantly higher risk of a plethora of illnesses and diseases, and in some cases death as a result of higher exposure to environmental health hazards.

Over 9 million people worldwide die every year from poor air quality. The United States has decreased air pollution deaths since the clean air act of 1963. Although our air is much cleaner since this bill, in 2020, estimates still associate 100,000 to 200,000 deaths with poor air quality. On top of premature death, a growing number of life-altering illnesses and diseases such as ischemic heart disease, stroke, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), lung cancer, and acute lower respiratory infections, are still present and growing. Unsurprisingly, the impacts of air and industrial pollution affect redlined communities at much higher rates. Flint, Michigan highlights similar structural issues, showing that water quality is not equal in all areas or among all races. With poor political protection, unfair zoning, and low mobility, these communities are trapped, gasping for clean air. By analyzing multiple cities and looking at human health data, we can find a clear link between the 1930s policy and health concerns. Lastly, we can build a case as to why this is happening, looking towards industrial zoning, highway proximity, low density of greenspaces, and more. After tackling the issue from a broader perspective, diving into the issue at home, here in Austin, will be useful for showing that these issues exist in our backyard and that the impacts are visible and measurable.

Write our State representatives to advocate for change!

It is important for policymakers and community leaders in Austin and other cities to address the legacy of redlining and its impacts on environmental health. This can include investing in affordable housing and healthy food options in redlined neighborhoods, fixing superfund zoning, incentivizing cleaner transportation, testing water and air in these areas regularly, as well as implementing policies to prevent discrimination in the housing and lending markets. Only by addressing the root causes of inequality and poor health can we create a more equitable and healthy city for all residents of Austin.
